Introduction to Epidemiology

Origins · Definitions · Historical Evolution · Types · Uses · Key Terminology

Definition & Etymology

Greek roots: Epi = upon/among  |  Demos = people  |  Logos = study
→ "Study among people" — what befalls a population

Gordis (2009): Epidemiology is the study of how disease is distributed in populations and the factors that determine this distribution.
Last (1995): Epidemiology is the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related events/states in population groups, and the application of this study to the control of health problems.
🔑
Core premise: Disease is NOT randomly distributed in human populations — there are patterns and reasons why some people get sick and others don't.

Components of the Definition

💡
There are 6 components — your lecturer loves this topic. Know all six.
#ComponentMeaning
1PopulationFocuses on groups/communities, NOT individuals
2FrequencyQuantitative science — measures using rates/ratios; morbidity & mortality
3Health-related conditionsNot just disease — injuries, births, smoking, poverty, unemployment
4DistributionPatterns by time, place, and person
5DeterminantsCauses and risk factors that make people prone to disease
6ApplicationPrimary goal = identify prevention and control strategies
📌
The 3 core pillars: Disease Frequency (rates & ratios) · Distribution (time, place, person) · Determinants (cause / risk factors)

Distribution — Time, Place & Person

👤 Person

  • Age, gender, ethnicity
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Concurrent disease
  • Diet, exercise, smoking
  • Risk-taking behaviour
  • SES, education, occupation

📍 Place

  • Presence of agents / vectors
  • Climate, geology
  • Population density
  • Economic development
  • Nutritional practices
  • Medical practices

⏱ Time

  • Calendar time
  • Time since an event
  • Physiologic cycles
  • Age (time since birth)
  • Seasonality
  • Temporal trends

🔬 Analytic: Host, Agent, Env.

  • Descriptive: WHO? WHERE? WHEN?
  • Analytic: WHY? (host, agent, env.)
  • Person/Place/Time = description
  • Host/Agent/Environment = causation

Historical Evolution of Epidemiology

Period / PersonContribution
Hippocrates (~400 BC)First rational (not supernatural) explanation of disease. Essay: "On Airs, Waters, and Places" — environment and behaviour influence disease
John Graunt (1662)First to quantify patterns of birth, death & disease. Observed: male/female disparities, high infant mortality, urban/rural differences, seasonal variations
William Farr (mid-1800s)"Father of modern vital statistics & surveillance." Systematically collected Britain's mortality statistics. Studied effects of marital status, occupation & altitude.
John Snow (mid-1800s)"Father of field epidemiology." Investigated cholera in London before the microscope existed. Classic 1854 Golden Square study — mapped cases on a spot map, linked cholera to contaminated water pump.
Dr. Peter Panum (1846)Faroe Islands measles epidemic. Established ~14-day incubation period. Observed life-long immunity after surviving infection.
1930s–1940sMethods extended to non-infectious diseases. Doll & Hill: smoking → lung cancer. Framingham cardiovascular study.
1960s–1970sEpidemiology used to eradicate smallpox worldwide.
1980sMolecular & genetic epidemiology. New infectious agents: Ebola, Marburg, HIV/AIDS, Legionella, SARS, drug-resistant TB.
1990s–2001+Bioterrorism considered in epidemiologic planning post-9/11.
💡
John Snow's progression: Descriptive → Hypothesis → Analytic → Application — the gold standard approach still used today.

The Epidemiologic Triangle

Depicts the relationship among three key factors in disease occurrence:

🦠 Agent

  • Factor whose presence, absence, excess, or deficit is necessary for disease
  • Types: Biological, Physical, Chemical
  • Necessary for disease to occur

👤 Host

  • Person/animal harboring the agent
  • Personal traits, behaviours
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Immunologic factors
  • Influences severity & chance of disease

🌍 Environment

  • Social: Education, unemployment, culture, economics
  • Physical: Climate, terrain, radiation, pollution
  • Biological: Vectors, reservoirs, humans, plants
  • Contributes to the disease process
⚡
Epidemics arise when balance is disrupted: New agent · Change in agent (infectivity, pathogenicity, virulence) · Change in susceptible population · Environmental changes affecting transmission

Gradients of Infection

  1. Colonization — organism present without causing disease (e.g., S. aureus on skin/nasopharynx)
  2. Subclinical/inapparent infection — infection without symptoms (e.g., polio)
  3. Latent infection — dormant infection (e.g., herpes simplex virus)
  4. Manifest/clinical infection — symptomatic disease

Concepts & Principles of Public Health

Health Definitions · SDOH · Equity · Public Health Roles · Achievements

Definition of Health

WHO (1948): Health is "a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."

Why this matters: A significant shift from previous definitions that focused solely on the absence of illness. Established health as a holistic, multi-dimensional concept. Foundational for World Health Day since 1948.

💡
Health is not just biological — it encompasses physical, mental, AND social dimensions.

Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)

DefinitionThe non-medical conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age that influence health outcomes.

Five Key SDOH Domains:

Economic Stability Education Access Healthcare Access Social & Community Context Neighbourhood & Built Environment

Why Do Some People Get Sick While Others Stay Healthy?

  • Biology/genetics
  • Poor diet
  • Exposure to hazards, conflicts
  • Behavioural factors (smoking, alcohol, physical inactivity)
  • Access to health services (how the state provides services)
  • Poverty, inequity, and poor living conditions
🌍
Illustrative example: In 2015, life expectancy in Sierra Leone = 50 years vs. Australia = 83 years — a 33-year difference largely explained by SDOH, not biology.

Health Equity, Inequity & Disparities

⚠️
These three terms are NOT the same — examiners love testing this distinction.
ConceptDefinition
Health EquityThe opportunity for everyone to attain their full health potential; no one is disadvantaged due to socially determined circumstances
Health InequitySystematic and unjust distribution of social, economic, and environmental conditions needed for health
Health DisparitiesDifferences in incidence/prevalence of health conditions between groups (race, SES, gender, geography, disability, sexual orientation)
⚖️
Equity ≠ Equality — Equity is about fairness and removing disadvantage; equality is about giving everyone the same thing regardless of need.

Definitions of Public Health

Oxford Textbook: "The process of mobilizing and engaging local, national and international health agencies and resources to assure the conditions in which people live."
American Heritage Dictionary: "The science and practice of protecting and improving the health of the community by health education, control of communicable diseases, application of sanitary measures and monitoring of environmental hazards."
Winslow (1920): "The science and art of preventing disease, prolonging life, promoting health and efficiency through organized community effort."

Primary Role of Public Health:

Disease Prevention Health Promotion Prolonging Life

Public Health vs. Clinical/Medical Approach

🌊
The River Analogy: Doctors are so busy pulling drowning people from the river that nobody goes upstream to find out why people are falling in. Public Health goes upstream.

Ten Essential Public Health Services

  1. Monitor health status to identify community health problems
  2. Diagnose and investigate health hazards in the community
  3. Inform, educate, and empower people about health issues

Overview of Disease Surveillance

Definitions · Types · IDSR · Outbreak Investigation · Key Terms

Definition of Surveillance

Surveillance is the continuous (ongoing) scrutiny of the factors that determine the occurrence and distribution of diseases and other health-related events through a systematic collection of data.
👁️
Surveillance = the eye and ear of health systems / public health actors

Surveillance systems = networks of people and activities maintaining this process, from local to international levels.

Components of Surveillance → Public Health Action

📥 Surveillance Activities

  • Data collection
  • Analysis
  • Interpretation
  • Dissemination

⚡ Resulting PH Action

  • Priority setting
  • Planning, implementing, evaluating
  • Investigation
  • Control & Prevention

Importance & Purpose of Surveillance

  • Early warning system for impending public health emergencies
  • Early detection of disease outbreaks
  • Differentiates endemic disease, localized outbreaks, epidemics, and pandemics
  • Documents impact of interventions / tracks progress toward goals
  • Understands disease trends over time
  • Guides public health interventions
  • Optimizes available public health resources
  • Provides baseline data to assess prevention/control measures

Levels of Surveillance

LevelDescriptionExample
Individual/FamilySurveillance of infected person who is a source to othersTyphoid carriers
Community/LocalSurveillance of whole community for early detection & controlMalaria
NationalSurveillance at national levelSmallpox post-eradication
InternationalWHO-listed diseases surveillanceMalaria, influenza

Procedure for Investigating an Outbreak

📋
This is a common long-answer exam question. Know all steps in order.
  1. Establish the existence of an outbreak — compare current to previous incidence in same community/season
  2. Confirm diagnosis — clinical histories + standard laboratory tests; determine the agent (bacterial, viral, fungal, other)
  3. Establish case definition and count cases — develop a case definition; establish counting methods
  4. Relate to time, place, and person — interview known cases: when ill (time), where infected (place), who they are (person)
  5. Determine who is at risk — count cases, relate to groups at risk; contact those with info on illnesses/environment
  6. Formulate a tentative hypothesis — explain most likely cause, source, and distribution of cases
  7. Compare hypothesis with established facts — test hypothesis with gathered data
  8. Plan detailed epidemiologic investigation — determine additional info needed; develop interview forms; gather sampling equipment
  9. Gather community and environmental information — investigate sources; obtain samples; summarize and interpret all data and lab results
  10. Construct epidemic curves; apply statistical tests — calculate rates; accept or reject hypothesis
  11. Prepare a written report — summarize investigation as soon as completed
  12. Implement control and prevention measures — use evidence gathered; initiate ongoing surveillance

Outbreak · Epidemic · Pandemic · Cluster

TermMeaning
OutbreakCases exceeding expected numbers in a defined area/time; same as epidemic but less alarming connotation
EpidemicSame as outbreak; used for more serious situations (avoid to prevent panic)
PandemicEpidemic widespread geographically — national, continental, or global
ClusterGroup of cases in a specific time and place — may or may not exceed normal rates
EndemicDisease with high background rate in a given place/time; constantly present

Global Health Threats & Strategies

10 Major Threats · Cross-Cutting Strategies · Key Facts
📌
For each threat know: what it is, why it matters, and what strategies address it. The table at the end summarizes all 10.

Threat 1 — Pandemic Diseases

Large-scale outbreaks causing significant morbidity, mortality, economic, social, and political disruption over a wide geographic area (e.g., COVID-19).

Strategies:

  • Strengthening surveillance and response systems
  • Investing in research for vaccines and treatments
  • Enhancing global cooperation and information sharing
  • Investing in core public health infrastructure (water, sanitation)
  • Increasing situational awareness
  • Rapidly extinguishing early sparks before they become pandemics

Threat 2 — Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

Rise of resistant bacteria making common infections harder or impossible to treat. Threatens food safety, economic development, equity, and the SDGs. Demands urgent action.

🔑
AMR is a One Health issue — affects humans, animals, plants, food, and the environment simultaneously.

Strategies:

  • Promoting responsible antibiotic use in healthcare and agriculture
  • Supporting research for new antibiotics and alternative therapies
  • Implementing robust infection control measures

Threat 3 — War and Conflicts

Major cause of disability worldwide. Disrupts healthcare systems, displaces populations, creates hostile environments for healthcare delivery. Leads to communicable diseases, malnutrition, and physical injuries.

Strategies:

  • Supporting rebuilding of healthcare facilities in conflict-affected areas
  • Providing emergency aid and medical assistance to displaced populations

Threat 4 — Agriculture and Animal Health

Human-animal interactions, biodiversity erosion, ecosystem alteration, and climate change drive emergence of infectious diseases — most originating from animals.

🐾
Zoonotic diseases affect more than 2 billion people and cause over 2 million deaths every year.

Strategies:

  • Integrated, multidisciplinary solutions to prevent, detect, and manage risks
  • One Health approach — collaboration among food production, processing, and distribution stakeholders
  • Improved surveillance, monitoring, and risk assessment for foodborne illnesses

Threat 5 — Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)

Not spread through infection — caused by unhealthy behaviours. Leading cause of death worldwide.

❤️
The Big Four NCDs account for over 2/3 of global deaths: Cardiovascular diseases · Cancers · Diabetes · Chronic respiratory diseases
✅
80% of heart diseases, strokes, and type 2 diabetes + 1/3 of cancers can be prevented by: cutting tobacco · healthy diet · physical activity · stopping harmful alcohol use

Strategies:

  • Promoting healthy lifestyles through public education campaigns
  • Policies encouraging physical activity and healthy eating
  • Increasing access to preventive healthcare services
  • Strengthening health systems and universal health coverage
  • Reducing modifiable risk factors: tobacco, alcohol, unhealthy diets, inactivity
  • International cooperation and advocacy; robust monitoring of trends

Threat 6 — Health Inequities

⚠️
At least 50% of the world's population lacks access to essential healthcare. Nearly 100 million people pushed into extreme poverty annually due to uninsured medical costs.

Chronic global shortage of health workers compounded by migration from developing to developed countries. Developing countries face challenges in financing, workforce, infrastructure, supplies, and health information systems.

Strategies:

  • Implementing policies that target underserved populations
  • Promoting social determinants of health (housing, education, income)
  • Increasing community engagement in health initiatives
  • Strengthening Primary Health Care (PHC) systems, especially where physical access is a barrier

Threat 7 — Environmental Factors & Climate Change

🌡️
WHO identifies climate change as the most significant health threat facing humanity in the intermediate term.

Environmental factors: air pollution, water pollution, toxic chemicals, climate change, natural disasters. Over 100 illnesses and injuries directly linked to environmental health. Greatest impact on poor communities.

Climate health effects: More infectious diseases emerging (dengue, Ebola) · Disease vector suitability increased ~10% between 1950 and 2018 · More frequent hurricanes, flooding, drought, heat waves · Heat waves → respiratory/cardiovascular disease, reduced food access, higher mortality.

Strategies:

  • Integrating climate change into public health planning and policy
  • Promoting sustainability and reducing carbon footprints
  • Advanced technologies to reduce pollution
  • Strict environmental regulations (e.g., EU Air Quality Directive)
  • Investment in public health infrastructure

Threat 8 — Global Migration and Displacement

Displaced populations live in poor sanitary conditions with inadequate shelter, crowding, and suboptimal nutrition — increasing risk of communicable diseases.

Strategies:

  • Developing inclusive health systems for migrants and refugees
  • Enhancing vaccination and preventive measures in migrant communities
  • Facilitating access to healthcare regardless of immigration status

Threat 9 — Mental Health and Substance Abuse

🧠
Post-pandemic, mental health incidence is higher than cancer. Historically underfunded and isolated from conventional healthcare.

Drivers of anxiety/depression epidemic: rising cost of living, ongoing global conflicts, climate change. Alcohol, tobacco, and drugs associated with cancer, chronic lung/liver disease, STIs, and mental disorders.

Strategies:

  • Integrate mental health throughout the healthcare continuum
  • Scale up evidence-based harm reduction interventions
  • Digital health interventions, collaborative care models, peer support programs
  • Holistic approach targeting: stigma, poverty, unemployment, violence, conflict

Threat 10 — Emerging and Re-emerging Infectious Diseases

Significant obstacles in poor communities. Socioeconomic determinants and inequity influence occurrence and spread. Malaria disproportionately affects the poorest and rural populations in Sub-Saharan Africa. HIV/AIDS and malaria continue to put massive pressure on healthcare systems.

Strategies:

  • Improving quality of primary health services as foundation for prevention
  • Adequate financial support to public health infrastructure
  • Working toward alleviating poverty
  • Interagency and international collaboration for research, surveillance, and response

Morbidity — Definition

MorbidityAny departure, subjective or objective, from a state of physical and psychological well-being. In practice encompasses disease, injury, and disability. Also refers to the number of persons ill, periods of illness, or duration of illness.

Two key measures: Incidence (who becomes ill) and Prevalence (who is ill at a given time).

Incidence

The number of new cases of disease in a defined population over a defined period of time.

Incidence Formula
Incidence = New cases in population over defined time / Persons at risk during that time

Key Properties:

  • Only includes new cases in numerator
  • Denominator = persons at risk at start of observation period
  • Incidence proportion = measure of risk or probability of developing disease
  • Denominator must be restricted to population at risk (e.g., ovarian cancer denominator = women only)
📝
Worked Example: 100 of 189 diabetic men died during 13-year follow-up.
Risk = 100/189 × 100 = 52.9%
📝
Attack Rate Example: 30 of 99 persons who ate potato salad developed gastroenteritis.
Attack rate = 30/99 × 100 = 30.3%

Prevalence

The proportion of a population who have a particular disease at a specified point or over a specified period of time.

Prevalence Formula
Prevalence = Persons with disease / Total persons in population

Point Prevalence

Prevalence at a single point in time — a snapshot. Proportion with disease at that moment.

Period Prevalence

Prevalence measured over an interval of time — covers both existing and new cases during the period.

Key Relationship
Prevalence = Incidence × Duration of disease
📝
Worked Example: 200 of 4,000 surveyed people in Tana River district positive for schistosomiasis.
Prevalence = 200/4,000 × 100 = 5%

Incidence vs. Prevalence — Key Differences

FeatureIncidencePrevalence
What it countsNew cases onlyAll existing cases (new + pre-existing)
MeasuresRisk of developing diseaseBurden of disease
Best forAcute diseases, identifying causesChronic diseases, resource planning
DenominatorPopulation at risk at startPopulation during same time period
💡
High prevalence may reflect: high incidence OR prolonged survival without cure OR both
Low prevalence may indicate: low incidence OR rapidly fatal disease OR rapid recovery
Prevalence preferred for chronic diseases (diabetes) — long duration, difficult date of onset

Relative Risk (RR)

Ratio of risk comparing two groups based on exposure status. Measures strength of association. Used in cohort (prospective) studies.

RR Formula
RR = [a / (a+b)] ÷ [c / (c+d)] = Incidence(exposed) / Incidence(unexposed)
RR ValueInterpretation
RR = 1.0No association — risk identical in exposed and unexposed
RR > 1.0Positive association — increased risk in exposed group
RR < 1.0Negative association — decreased risk in exposed (protective)
RR = 5Exposed are 5 times more likely to have the outcome
RR = 0.5Exposed are half as likely to have the outcome
RR = 1.330% increased risk for exposed vs. unexposed
📝
E. coli & pink hamburger:
Ate hamburger: 23 ill / 33 total  |  Did not eat: 7 ill / 67 total
RR = (23/33) ÷ (7/67) = 6.67 — those who ate the hamburger were 6.67× more likely to develop E. coli.

Odds Ratio (OR)

Ratio of the odds of disease in exposed vs. unexposed persons. Used in case-control studies (population at risk not known).

OR Formula
OR = (a × d) / (b × c)
📌
OR is interpreted the same way as RR: = 1 (no association) · >1 (positive) · <1 (negative/protective)
📝
MMR vaccine & autism:
OR = (130 × 135) / (115 × 120) = 17,550 / 13,800 = 1.27 — very weak association, not significant.

Attributable Risk (AR) / Risk Difference (RD)

The excess risk of disease in the exposed group directly attributable to the exposure, after accounting for background risk.

AR Formula (also called Risk Difference or Excess Risk)
AR = I(exposed) − I(unexposed)
📝
Fast vs. slow driving:
Fast risk = 0.050  |  Slow risk = 0.010
AR = 0.050 − 0.010 = 0.040 (4 extra deaths per 100 due to fast driving)

Attributable Fraction (AF%) / Attributable Risk %

AR expressed as a percentage of total risk in exposed group. Answers: what proportion of disease among exposed is due to the exposure?

Also called: Attributable proportion · Etiologic fraction

AF% Formula
AF% = [I(exposed) − I(unexposed)] / I(exposed) × 100 Also: AF% = 1 − (1/RR) Case-control: AF = 1 − (1/OR)
📝
Fast driving: (0.050 − 0.010) / 0.050 × 100 = 80%
Drunk driving: (0.150 − 0.014) / 0.150 × 100 = 91%

Prevented Fraction (PF) — Vaccine Efficacy

Used when exposure is associated with decreased risk (RR < 1, e.g., a vaccine). Answers: what proportion of potential cases were prevented by the exposure?

📝
Fluoridation of drinking water:
Fluoridated area = 14.8% dental caries  |  Non-fluoridated = 21.3%
PF = (21.3 − 14.8) / 21.3 = 30.5% of caries prevented by fluoridation.

Population Attributable Risk (PAR) & Fraction (PAF)

PAR = excess risk of disease in the total population attributable to exposure.
PAF = proportion of cases in total population attributable to exposure, as % of total population risk.
Key use: Helps determine which exposures to target for greatest population-level impact.

PAF in Case-Control Studies
PAF = (1 − 1/OR) × Pc where Pc = % of cases who were exposed
🚗
Driving-related deaths conclusion:
44% attributed to fast driving · 22% attributed to drunk driving
→ Targeting fast driving would have a greater population-level impact

Tests of Statistical Significance

95% Confidence Interval (CI)

  • Range of values with 95% chance of containing the true RR or OR
  • To be significant: CI must NOT include 1.0
  • CI entirely above 1 → increased risk
  • CI entirely below 1 → decreased risk (protective)
  • CI includes 1 → no significant association

p-value

  • Probability that observed association occurred by chance alone
  • p < 0.05 → statistically significant (less than 5% chance it's due to chance)
  • Very small p-value → very unlikely by chance alone

Mortality Measures

Crude Death Rate
Total deaths during interval / Mid-interval population × 1,000
Cause-Specific Death Rate
Deaths from specific cause / Mid-interval population × 1,000

Case Fatality Rate (CFR) — "Killing Power of a Disease"

CFRA measure of the severity of a disease — proportion of diagnosed cases who die.
CFR Formula
CFR = Deaths from disease in given period / Diagnosed cases in same period × 100
📝
Malaria (Siaya, 1998): 20 deaths / 200 admitted = CFR = 10%
Ebola (West Africa, peak): 90 deaths / 100 admitted = CFR = 90%
→ Ebola is far more severe

Person-Time

A unit combining persons and time for calculating incidence rates when individuals are followed for different lengths of time.

1 person × 1 year = 1 person-year 1 person × 1 month = 1 person-month Units: year⁻¹ or month⁻¹

Used in incidence density calculations, especially in cohort studies.
