Introduction to Epidemiology

Origins · Definitions · Historical Evolution · Distribution · Types · Uses · Disease Causation · Epidemiologic Triangle · Key Terminology · Infection

1. Definition & Etymology

Etymology (Greek roots):
Epi = upon/among  |  Demos = people  |  Logos = study
→ "Study among people" — what befalls a population

Two Key Definitions to Know:

Gordis (2009): Epidemiology is the study of how disease is distributed in populations and the factors that determine this distribution.
Last (1995): Epidemiology is the study of the distribution and determinants of health-related events/states in population groups, and the application of this study to the control of health problems.
🔑
Core premise: Disease is NOT randomly distributed in human populations — there are patterns and reasons why some people get sick and others don't.

2. Components of the Definition

⚠️
There are 6 components — memorize all of them.
#ComponentMeaning
1PopulationFocuses on groups/communities, NOT individuals
2FrequencyQuantitative science — measures disease using rates/ratios (morbidity & mortality)
3Health-related conditionsNot just disease — includes injuries, births, smoking, poverty, unemployment
4DistributionPatterns by time, place, and person (who, where, when)
5DeterminantsCauses and risk factors that make people prone to disease
6ApplicationPrimary goal = identify prevention and control strategies

The 3 core pillars (frequently summarized as):

  1. Disease frequency (rates & ratios)
  2. Distribution of disease (time, place, person)
  3. Determinants of disease (cause/risk factors)

3. Distribution — Time, Place, Person

👤 Person — who is affected?

  • Age, gender, ethnicity
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Concurrent disease
  • Diet, exercise, smoking
  • Risk-taking behavior
  • SES (socioeconomic status), education, occupation

📍 Place — where does it occur?

  • Presence of agents/vectors
  • Climate, geology
  • Population density
  • Economic development
  • Nutritional & medical practices

⏱ Time — when does it occur?

  • Calendar time
  • Time since an event
  • Physiologic cycles
  • Age (time since birth)
  • Seasonality
  • Temporal trends

4. History / Historical Evolution

Hippocrates (~400 BC)

  • Attempted to explain disease from a rational, not supernatural, viewpoint
  • Essay: "On Airs, Waters, and Places" — suggested environment and behavior influence disease

John Graunt (1662)

  • First to quantify patterns of birth, death, and disease
  • Observed: male-female disparities, high infant mortality, urban/rural differences, seasonal variations

William Farr (mid-1800s)

  • "Father of modern vital statistics and surveillance"
  • Systematically collected and analyzed Britain's mortality statistics
  • Studied effects of marital status, occupation, and altitude on disease
  • Developed many epidemiologic concepts still used today

John Snow (mid-1800s) — "Father of Field Epidemiology"

  • An anesthesiologist (not a microbiologist — 20 years before the microscope)
  • Investigated cholera outbreaks in London
  • Classic progression: descriptive → hypothesis → analytic
  • Key study: Golden Square Cholera Epidemic (1854)
    • Mapped cases using a spot map
    • Identified contaminated water from the Broad Street pump as the source
    • Compared cholera death rates in areas supplied by Lambeth Company vs Southwark & Vauxhall Company
    • Lambeth changed their water source (1849–1854) → cholera declined in their area; no change in Southwark & Vauxhall areas
    • Concluded: cholera = transmitted by contaminated water

Dr. Peter Ludwig Panum (1846 — Faroe Islands)

  • Studied measles epidemic in Faroe Islands
  • Key observations:
    1. Measles attacked all age groups (not just children) — because of long isolation
    2. Rash appeared ~14 days after exposure (established incubation period)
    3. Surviving infection = life-long immunity

19th–20th Century Milestones:

PeriodDevelopment
Mid-1800sEpidemiologic methods applied to infectious disease investigation
1930s–1940sMethods extended to non-infectious diseases (Doll & Hill: smoking → lung cancer; Framingham cardiovascular study)
1960s–1970sEpidemiology used to eradicate smallpox worldwide
1980sRise of molecular & genetic epidemiology; new agents: Ebola, Marburg, HIV/AIDS, Legionella, SARS, drug-resistant TB
1990s–2001+Bioterrorism considered; deliberate spread of biological agents
TodayEpidemiology used routinely in public health worldwide

In Health Care Management:

  • Planning and evaluation of health services
  • Developing health policies

In Understanding Disease:

  • Searching for causes and risk factors
  • Historical studies of the rise and fall of disease
  • Identification of syndromes

  • Surveillance of disease
  • Making projections for the future
  • Formulating medical teaching curricula

  • Assisting in clinical decision-making

Four Core Uses (Summary for Exams):

  2. Causation — identifying what causes disease
  3. Evaluation of interventions — does prevention/treatment work?

7. Disease Frequency

Rate — signifies the speed of frequency of occurrence per unit of time

  • Numerator is part of the denominator
  • Example: Notification rate = New smear-positive TB cases / 100,000 population
  • Cure rate = Cases cured / Cases under treatment × 100

Incidence — number of new cases in a given time period

  • Expressed as % infected/year (cumulative incidence) or number per person-time (incidence density)

Prevalence — number of cases at a given time

  • Prevalence = Incidence × Duration of disease
  • Less useful for short-duration diseases (e.g., measles)
  • More useful for long-duration diseases (e.g., chronic hepatitis B)

8. Disease Causation

Cause of disease: An event, condition, characteristic, or combination of these that plays an important role in producing disease.

Two Types of Causes:

TypeDefinitionExample
Primary (etiologic) causeNecessary for disease to occur; disease will NOT occur in its absenceM. tuberculosis for pulmonary TB
Risk factorsNot necessarily causes but important for disease occurrence; associated with increased OR decreased occurrenceSmoking (risk factor for lung cancer)

Etiology = sum total of all primary causes + risk factors contributing to disease occurrence.

9. The Epidemiologic Triangle

The triangle depicts the relationship among three key factors in disease occurrence:

Epidemiologic Triangle
AGENT / \ / \ HOST ——— ENVIRONMENT
ComponentDefinitionExamples
AgentFactor whose presence, absence, excess, or deficit is necessary for disease/injuryBiological, physical, chemical agents
HostPerson/animal where infectious agent lives; factors affecting susceptibilityAge, sex, genetics, immunity, nutrition, occupation
EnvironmentAll external factors (other than agent) that influence healthSocial, physical, biological environments

Social environment: Education, unemployment, culture, diet, transport, legal/economic/health systems

Physical environment: Climate, terrain, radiation, pollution

Biological environment: Vectors, disease reservoirs, humans, plants

Epidemics arise when balance is disrupted:

  • New agent introduced
  • Change in existing agent (infectivity, pathogenicity, virulence)
  • Change in number of susceptibles in the population
  • Environmental changes affecting transmission or agent growth

Host factors — influence chance for disease or severity: personal traits, behaviors, genetic predisposition, immunologic factors

Agent types: Biological / Physical / Chemical — necessary for disease to occur

Primary vs. Secondary Cases

  • Primary (index) case — the person who introduces infection into a population
  • Secondary cases — those who contract infection subsequently
  • Further spread described as "waves" or "generations"

11. Infection & Related Terms

Infection — entry and development/multiplication of an infectious agent in the body; does NOT always cause illness.

Gradients of infection (levels):

  1. Colonization — organism present without causing disease (e.g., S. aureus on skin/nasopharynx)
  2. Subclinical/inapparent infection — infection without symptoms (e.g., polio)
  3. Latent infection — dormant infection (e.g., herpes simplex virus)
  4. Manifest/clinical infection — symptomatic disease

Contamination — presence of an infectious agent on body surface, clothing, bedding, toys, instruments, water, or food

Infestation — lodgment, development, and reproduction of:

  • Arthropods on body surface or clothing (e.g., lice, itch mite)
  • Parasitic worms in the gut (e.g., ascariasis)

Contagious disease — transmitted through direct contact (e.g., scabies, trachoma, STIs, leprosy)

12. Host, Reservoir & Vector

Host — person or animal that affords subsistence/lodgement to an infectious agent under natural conditions

Types of hosts: Obligate host · Definitive (primary) host · Intermediate host · Transport host

Reservoir — natural habitat of the infectious agent; where it normally lives, multiplies, and depends for survival (can be person, animal, arthropod, plant, soil, or substance)

Vector — insect or living carrier that transports an infectious agent from infected individual to susceptible individual or their food/surroundings

  • Biological transmission — agent multiplies in vector
  • Mechanical transmission — agent carried passively (no multiplication)

13. Additional Terminology

Concepts & Principles of Public Health and Epidemiology

Health Definitions · SDOH · Equity · Public Health Roles · Core Functions · Achievements · Organizations

1. Definition of Health

WHO Definition (1948): Health is "a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity."

Why this definition matters:

  • Found in the Preamble to the WHO Constitution
  • A significant shift — previous definitions focused solely on the absence of illness
  • Established health as a holistic, multi-dimensional concept
  • Foundational for World Health Day since 1948
🔑
Key point: Health is not just biological — it encompasses physical, mental, AND social dimensions.

2. Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)

DefinitionThe non-medical conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age that influence health outcomes.

Five Key SDOH Domains:

  1. Economic stability (income, employment, poverty)
  2. Education access (literacy, early childhood education)
  3. Healthcare access (availability, quality, affordability)
  4. Social and community context (social support, discrimination)
  5. Neighbourhood and built environment (housing, sanitation, transport)

What "Social" Means in SDOH:

  • Social environment — where we live
  • Social identity — the group we identify with
  • SES (Socioeconomic Status) — money gives options and discretionary resources; inequality = unequal distribution of goods and opportunities based on identity

Why Do Some People Get Sick While Others Stay Healthy?

  • Biology/genetics
  • Poor diet
  • Exposure to hazards, conflicts
  • Behavioral factors (smoking, alcohol, physical inactivity)
  • Access to health services
  • Poverty and inequity
  • Poor living conditions
  • Not always about biology — social setting matters equally
🌍
Illustrative Example: In 2015, a child born in Sierra Leone had a life expectancy of 50 years, while a child born in Australia could expect to live 83 years — a difference of 33 years, largely explained by SDOH.

3. Health Equity, Inequity, and Disparities

⚠️
These three terms are not the same — examiners love testing this.
ConceptDefinition
Health EquityThe opportunity for everyone to attain their full health potential; no one is disadvantaged due to socially determined circumstances
Health InequitySystematic and unjust distribution of social, economic, and environmental conditions needed for health
Health DisparitiesDifferences in incidence/prevalence of health conditions between groups

Causes of Health Inequities:

  • Unequal access to quality education, healthcare, housing, transportation
  • Unequal employment opportunities and income/pay
  • Discrimination based on social status or other attributes

Bases of Health Disparities:

Race Socioeconomic status Sexual orientation Gender Disability status Geographical location Combination of the above
⚖️
Equity ≠ Equality — Equity is about fairness and removing disadvantage; equality is about giving everyone the same thing regardless of need.

4. Definitions of Public Health

SourceDefinition
Oxford Textbook of Public Health"The process of mobilizing and engaging local, national and international health agencies and resources to assure the conditions in which people live"
American Heritage Dictionary"The science and practice of protecting and improving the health of the community by health education, control of communicable diseases, application of sanitary measures and monitoring of environmental hazards"
Winslow (1920)"The science and art of preventing disease, prolonging life, promoting health and efficiency through organized community effort"
📌
Common thread: Public health = population-level, organized, preventive effort.

Primary Role of Public Health:

  1. Disease prevention
  2. Health promotion
  3. Prolonging life

5. Threats to Public Health

  • Infectious disease outbreaks (influenza, Ebola, malaria, dengue)
  • Chronic illnesses (heart disease, cancers, diabetes)
  • Environmental disasters (hurricanes, tornadoes, earthquakes)
  • Biological or chemical terrorism
  • Poor sanitary and environmental conditions

6. Core Activities of Public Health

Prevention & Protection:

  • Prevention of epidemics and spread of disease
  • Protection against environmental hazards
  • Prevention of injuries

Response & Promotion:

  • Response to disasters and assisting communities in recovery
  • Promotion of healthy behaviours

Systems & Research:

  • Assurance of quality, accessibility, and accountability of health services
  • Monitoring health status of the population
  • Mobilizing community action
  • Reaching out to link high-risk/hard-to-reach people to services
  • Researching for new insights and innovative solutions
  • Leading development of sound health policy and planning

9. Public Health Approach (The 5-Step Framework)

This is a cycle — memorize the order:

5-Step Framework
Surveillance → Risk Factor Identification → Intervention → Implementation → Evaluation

Applied to John Snow's Cholera Study (1854) — Classic Exam Case:

StepWhat Snow Did
SurveillanceIdentified cluster of cholera cases in Golden Square, London
Risk Factor IDMapped cases on a spot map; identified contaminated water pumps
InterventionDetermined need to stop exposure to contaminated water supply
ImplementationConvinced British government that cholera came from sewage-contaminated water
EvaluationAssessed decline of cholera cases after removing pump handle
💡
John Snow = "Father of Modern Epidemiology" — traced cholera source before the microscope was developed

Public Health Methods for Addressing Problems:

  • Legislation — e.g., mandatory seatbelts, helmets
  • Fiscal policy — e.g., taxing alcohol and tobacco
  • Social initiatives — e.g., literacy programmes, housing schemes
  • Disease prevention programmes — e.g., immunization

11. Determinants of Health

Health is a multi-dimensional concept that transcends mere absence of disease.

Levels of Determinants:

  1. General conditions: Socioeconomic, political, cultural, and environmental conditions
  2. Living and working conditions: Housing, healthcare services, employment/unemployment, education, water and sanitation, agriculture/food
  3. Social/community networks: Family support · Participation in government processes
  4. Individual and lifestyle factors: Age, sex, and constitutional (genetic) factors · Traditions and beliefs · Socioeconomic status → determines life expectancy · Occupational risks and stress
  5. Unemployment — can lead to: financial instability · inaccessibility to health and other services

Overview of Disease Surveillance

Definitions · Importance · Levels · Types · Sources · IDSR · Outbreak Investigation · Key Terminology

1. Definition of Surveillance

Surveillance is the continuous (ongoing) scrutiny of the factors that determine the occurrence and distribution of diseases and other health-related events through a systematic collection of data.

Expanded definition: An information-based activity involving collection, analysis, and interpretation of large volumes of health data from a variety of sources — used to evaluate control interventions and detect emergence of new infections.

Simple working definition: The process used to collect, manage, analyze, interpret, and report information on disease status.

👁️
Surveillance = the eye and ear of health systems/public health actors

Surveillance systems = networks of people and activities that maintain this process, functioning from local to international levels.

2. Components of Surveillance → Public Health Action

Surveillance Activities→ Resulting Public Health Action
Data collectionPriority setting
AnalysisPlanning, implementing, evaluating disease programs
InterpretationInvestigation
DisseminationControl and Prevention

3. Importance / Purpose of Surveillance

Why Surveillance Matters:

  • Early warning system for impending public health emergencies
  • Early detection of disease outbreaks
  • Differentiates between endemic disease, localized outbreaks, widespread epidemics, and global pandemics
  • Documents impact of an intervention or tracks progress toward goals
  • Understands disease trends over time
  • Guides public health interventions
  • Optimizes available public health resources
  • Monitors and clarifies epidemiology to allow priorities to be set and policy to be informed

Two Additional Justifications:

  1. Learn more about natural history, clinical spectrum, and epidemiology of a disease — who is at risk, when/where it occurs, exposures/risk factors
  2. Provide a baseline of data to assess prevention and control measures once implemented

4. Who Performs Surveillance?

Epidemiologists Medical professionals (doctors, nurses, clinics) Pharmacies Health insurance providers Emergency responders (9-1-1 centers, EMTs) Public health departments (local, state, federal)

6. Types of Surveillance (Major Exam Topic!)

6.1 Passive Surveillance

Definition: Routine surveillance based on passive case detection — information providers come to health institutions; data collected as part of routine health service provision.

AdvantagesDisadvantages/Limitations
Covers wide range of problemsInformation largely unreliable, incomplete, inaccurate
No special arrangement requiredData often not available on time
Relatively cheapMay not give desired type of information
Covers wide areaLacks representativeness — only captures people visiting facilities
—Limited to population segment visiting health facilities

6.2 Active Surveillance

Definition: Data collection usually on a specific disease for a relatively limited time period; involves house-to-house surveys or mobilizing communities to a central point; health personnel assigned to collect information at regular intervals.

AdvantagesDisadvantages/Limitations
Data is complete and accurateRequires good organization
Information collected is timelyExpensive
—Requires skilled human power
—Short period only (not continuous)
—Directed toward specific diseases only

When to Use Active Surveillance:

  • Periodic evaluation of an ongoing program
  • Programs with limited time of operation (e.g., eradication programs)
  • Unusual situations:
    • New disease discovery
    • New mode of transmission
    • Disease affecting a new subgroup of the population
    • Previously eradicated disease reappears

6.3 Sentinel Surveillance

Definition: Selected health facilities (sentinel sites) monitor and collect diseases of interest to supplement routine notification systems and identify missing cases.

Criteria for Selecting a Sentinel Site:

  • Willing to participate
  • Serves a relatively high population
  • Medical personnel sufficiently trained to diagnose, treat, and report cases
AdvantagesDisadvantages
Rational — less but better dataRepresentativeness issues
Consistent data (stable sample)Denominator may be unknown
Good data quality, timelinessVoluntary participation may be inconsistent
Geographical distribution considered—

6.4 Syndromic Surveillance

Definition: Focuses on one symptom or constellation of symptoms (clinical outcomes) rather than a confirmed/diagnosed disease.

  • Relatively new surveillance method
  • Uses clinical information before diagnosis is made
  • Often uses electronic data from hospital emergency rooms
  • No laboratory confirmation required
  • More sensitive, but less specific
  • Enables faster public health intervention

11. Conducting Surveillance — Notifiable Diseases

Diseases notifiable by law — regulations typically specify:

  • Which diseases/conditions must be reported
  • Who is responsible for reporting
  • What information is required per case
  • How, to whom, and how quickly the information is to be reported
  • Control measures to be taken for specified diseases

Two Additional Reportable Circumstances:

  1. Any outbreak or unusually high incidence of any disease
  2. Any occurrence of an unusual disease of public health importance

Who is Obligated to Report:

  • Physicians, dentists, nurses, and other health professionals
  • Medical examiners
  • Administrators of hospitals, clinics, nursing homes, schools, nurseries

Principle: Combines activities by taking advantage of similar surveillance functions, skills, resources, and target populations.

14. Disease Outbreaks

Definition (CDC): The occurrence of cases of disease in excess of what would normally be expected in a defined community, geographical area, or season over a given period of time.

📌
Important note: A single case of a communicable disease long absent from a population, or caused by a previously unrecognized agent, may also constitute an outbreak and should be reported.

Outbreak vs. Epidemic vs. Pandemic vs. Cluster:

TermMeaning
OutbreakCases exceeding expected numbers in a defined area/time; same as epidemic but less alarming connotation
EpidemicSame as outbreak; used for more serious situations
PandemicEpidemic widespread geographically — national, continental, or global
ClusterGroup of cases in a specific time and place — may or may not exceed normal rates
EndemicDisease with high background rate in a given place and time; constantly present

15. Procedure for Investigating an Outbreak

📋
Likely Exam Essay Question — Know all steps in order.
  1. Establish the existence of an outbreak — compare current data to previous incidence in the same community/season
  2. Confirm diagnosis — analyze clinical histories; standard laboratory tests to confirm the suspected diagnosis and determine the agent (bacterial, viral, fungal, other)
  3. Establish the case definition and count cases — develop a case definition; establish methods for counting cases
  4. Relate the outbreak to time, place, and person — interview known cases: when they became ill (time), where infected (place), who they are (person)
  5. Determine who is at risk — count cases and relate to groups at risk; contact those who can provide information on illnesses or environmental conditions
  6. Formulate a tentative hypothesis — explain the most likely cause, source, and distribution of cases
  7. Compare hypothesis with established facts — hypothesis directs the investigation and is tested by gathered data
  8. Plan a detailed epidemiologic investigation — determine what other information is needed; develop interview forms; gather sampling equipment; gather information from people who had similar exposures but are NOT ill
  9. Gather environmental and community information — investigate potential sources; obtain samples; summarize and interpret all information and laboratory results
  10. Construct epidemic curves; apply statistical tests — calculate rates; accept or reject hypothesis based on data
  11. Prepare a written report — summarize the investigation as soon as it is completed
  12. Implement control and prevention measures — use evidence gathered to control the current outbreak; initiate ongoing surveillance

Global Health Threats Likely to Affect Public Health Practice

10 Major Threats · Core Problems · Strategies · Cross-Cutting Themes

Overview

This unit covers 10 major global health threats and the strategies to address each one. For each threat, know: what it is, why it matters, and what strategies are used.

Threat 1 — Pandemic Diseases

What it is: Large-scale outbreaks of infectious disease that greatly increase morbidity and mortality over a wide geographic area, causing significant economic, social, and political disruption.

Why it matters:

  • Infectious diseases like COVID-19 spread rapidly, overwhelming healthcare systems
  • Can affect entire nations, continents, or the world simultaneously

Strategies:

  • Strengthening surveillance and response systems
  • Investing in research for vaccines and treatments
  • Enhancing global cooperation and information sharing
  • Most cost-effective approach (especially in resource-constrained settings):
    • Invest in core public health infrastructure (water and sanitation systems)
    • Increase situational awareness
    • Rapidly extinguish early sparks before they become pandemics

Threat 2 — Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

What it is: The rise of resistant bacteria, fungi, viruses, and parasites that make common infections harder or impossible to treat.

Why it matters:

  • Threatens our ability to treat human, animal, and plant diseases
  • Jeopardizes food safety and security
  • Threatens economic development and equity
  • Undermines progress toward the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
  • Demands urgent action

Strategies:

  • Promoting responsible antibiotic use in both healthcare and agriculture
  • Supporting research for new antibiotics and alternative therapies
  • Implementing robust infection control measures
🔑
AMR is a One Health issue — it affects humans, animals, plants, and the environment simultaneously.

Threat 3 — War and Conflicts

What it is: Armed conflict that causes death, disability, and widespread destruction of health infrastructure.

Why it matters:

  • Major cause of disability worldwide
  • Disrupts healthcare systems
  • Displaces populations
  • Creates hostile environments for healthcare delivery
  • Leads to increases in:
    • Communicable diseases
    • Malnutrition
    • Physical injuries

Strategies:

  • Supporting rebuilding of healthcare facilities in conflict-affected areas
  • Ensuring healthcare services are available to those in need
  • Providing emergency aid and medical assistance to displaced populations

Threat 4 — Agriculture and Animal Health

What it is: The intersection of animal–human interactions, biodiversity erosion, ecosystem alteration, and climate change driving the emergence of infectious diseases — most originating from animals.

Key Facts:

  • Zoonotic diseases (transmitted between animals and humans) affect more than 2 billion people and cause over 2 million deaths every year
  • Agriculture and food play a major role in disease emergence due to how food is produced, traded, and consumed
  • Foodborne diseases arise from poor hygiene and animal husbandry malpractices

Strategies:

  • Strong public health systems with integrated, multidisciplinary solutions to prevent, detect, and manage risks
  • One Health approach — collaboration and communication among all stakeholders in food production, processing, and distribution
  • Improved surveillance, monitoring, and risk assessment systems to prevent, detect, and respond to foodborne illnesses
  • Reduce food losses and ensure food safety
🔑
One Health = recognizing that human health, animal health, and environmental health are interconnected — must be addressed together.

Threat 5 — Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)

What they are: Diseases not spread through infection but typically caused by unhealthy behaviors. The leading cause of death worldwide.

The Big Four NCDs (account for over 2/3 of global deaths):

  1. Cardiovascular diseases
  2. Cancers
  3. Diabetes
  4. Chronic respiratory diseases
💡
Key Fact: 80% of heart diseases, strokes, and type 2 diabetes and over 1/3 of cancers can be prevented by: cutting out tobacco · eating a healthy diet · being physically active · stopping harmful use of alcohol

Strategies:

  • Promoting healthy lifestyles through public education campaigns
  • Implementing policies encouraging physical activity and healthy eating
  • Increasing access to preventive healthcare services
  • Strengthening health systems and developing public health policies and programs

Additional NCD Strategies (International Level):

  • Raise priority of NCDs through international cooperation and advocacy
  • Strengthen national capacity, governance, multisectoral action, and partnerships
  • Reduce major modifiable risk factors: tobacco, alcohol, unhealthy diets, physical inactivity
  • Develop and implement effective legal frameworks
  • Orient health systems through people-centered care and universal health coverage
  • Promote high-quality research and development
  • Monitor trends and determinants to achieve global, regional, and national targets

Threat 6 — Health Inequities

What it is: Unequal access to health services and outcomes based on geography, income, and social factors — worsening overall public health.

Key Facts:

  • At least 50% of the world's population does not have access to essential healthcare services
  • Nearly 100 million people are pushed into extreme poverty annually due to uninsured medical costs
  • Developing countries face challenges in: health financing, workforce development, infrastructure, supplies, and health information systems
  • Developed countries face gaps in: prescription drugs, long-term care, mental health, dental and eye care
  • Chronic global shortage of health workers — compounded by migration of workers from developing to developed countries

Strategies:

  • Implementing policies that target underserved populations
  • Promoting social determinants of health (housing, education, income)
  • Increasing community engagement in health initiatives
  • Strengthening Primary Health Care (PHC) systems, especially in regions where physical access (roads, transport) is a barrier

Threat 7 — Environmental Factors

What it is: A range of environmental conditions that directly affect health — including air pollution, water pollution, toxic chemicals, climate change, and natural disasters.

Health Impacts:

  • Respiratory problems
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Cancer
  • Malnutrition
  • Over 100 illnesses and injuries directly linked to environmental health factors (soil pollution, UV radiation, biodiversity loss)
  • Greatest impact on poor communities with existing healthcare vulnerabilities

Poor Water Quality — contributing factors:

  • Industrial waste and pollution
  • Lack of access to proper water treatment and sanitation
  • Outdated plumbing infrastructure

Climate Change — Special Emphasis:

🌡️
The WHO identifies climate change as the most significant health threat facing humanity in the intermediate term.

Health effects of climate change:

  • More infectious diseases emerging/reemerging (e.g., dengue in North & South America; Ebola in Africa)
  • Climate increasingly suitable for disease vectors — transmission increased by ~10% between 1950 and 2018
  • More frequent and severe environmental disasters: hurricanes, flooding, drought, heat waves
  • Heat waves associated with: respiratory & cardiovascular diseases, reduced food/nutrition access, higher mortality risk

Strategies:

  • Integrating climate change into public health planning and policy
  • Promoting sustainability and reducing carbon footprints
  • Enhancing preparedness for climate-related health impacts
  • Using advanced technologies to reduce pollution
  • Implementing strict environmental regulations (e.g., EU Air Quality Directive)
  • Investing in public health infrastructure

Threat 8 — Global Migration and Displacement

What it is: Large-scale movement of people (migrants, refugees, displaced persons) that strains health resources and spreads disease.

Why it matters:

  • Displaced populations live in poor sanitary conditions with inadequate shelter
  • Crowding and suboptimal nutrition increase risk of communicable diseases

Strategies:

  • Developing inclusive health systems that cater to migrants and refugees
  • Enhancing vaccination and preventive measures in migrant communities
  • Facilitating access to healthcare regardless of immigration status

Threat 9 — Mental Health and Substance Abuse

What it is: A rising global burden of mental health disorders and substance abuse historically neglected in health systems.

Key Facts:

  • Historically NOT a global health priority — overshadowed by communicable and non-communicable disease focus
  • Mental health management has been isolated from conventional healthcare with little funding
  • Post-pandemic, mental health incidence is higher than cancer
  • Drivers of the anxiety and depression epidemic:
    • Rising cost of living
    • Ongoing global conflicts
    • Impact of climate change
  • Alcohol, tobacco, and drugs are associated with: cancer, chronic lung and liver disease, STIs/blood-borne infections, and mental health disorders

Strategies:

  • Act toward inclusion and funding of mental health support programs
  • Integrate mental health throughout the healthcare continuum
  • Scale up funding and evidence-based harm reduction interventions
  • Increase access to treatment for substance abuse
  • New intervention models: collaborative care, digital health interventions, peer support programs
  • Community education, recovery support services, exercise and nutrition-based interventions
  • Holistic approach targeting underlying social/economic factors: stigma, poverty, unemployment, violence, conflict

Threat 10 — Emerging and Re-emerging Infectious Diseases

What it is: New or returning infectious diseases that present significant obstacles to health improvement, especially in poor communities.

Key Facts:

  • Socioeconomic determinants and inequity influence occurrence and spread
  • Poverty and limited access to clean water → spread of cholera, diarrheal diseases
  • HIV/AIDS and malaria continue to put massive pressure on healthcare systems
  • Malaria disproportionately affects the poorest and rural populations in Sub-Saharan Africa

Strategies:

  • Improving quality of primary health services — a resilient primary health care system is the foundation for preventing infectious diseases
  • Providing adequate financial support to public health infrastructure
  • Working toward alleviating poverty
  • Interagency and international collaboration for research, surveillance, and response
  • Shared goal of improving access to quality and affordable health services

Measures of Morbidity and Mortality in Epidemiology

Rate · Ratio · Proportion · Incidence · Prevalence · RR · OR · AR · AF% · PAR · CFR · Person-Time · Statistical Significance

1. Introduction & Basic Concepts

Why measurement matters:

  • Epidemiology focuses on measuring morbidity and mortality in human populations
  • Clear definitions minimize errors in data classification
  • Standardized methods of observation and recording are essential before any epidemiological study
  • Some areas remain hard to measure (e.g., social well-being)

What is measured in epidemiology:

  • Morbidity (illness)
  • Mortality (death)
  • Natality (births)
  • Impact measures
  • Disability
  • Disease characteristics (presence, absence, distribution)
  • Environmental factors suspected of causing disease
  • Demographic variables
  • Medical needs, healthcare facilities, and utilization of health services

2. Tools of Measurement — Rate, Ratio, and Proportion

⚠️
These three are the core tools. Know the differences cold — very likely exam question.

Rate

  • Measures the occurrence of an event (disease development or death) in a population during a given time period
  • A statement of risk of developing a condition
  • 4 components: Numerator + Denominator + Time specification + Multiplier
  • Numerator IS part of the denominator
  • Example: Death rate = (Number of deaths in a given year / Mid-year population) × 100,000

Ratio

  • Compares two dissimilar things by dividing one quantity by another
  • Numerator is NOT part of the denominator
  • Expressed as X/Y
  • Examples: Doctor-to-population ratio, male-to-female ratio, Maternal Mortality Ratio
  • Example: In a class of 30 students with 10 males → Male ratio = 10/30

Proportion

  • Numerator IS included in the denominator (same as rate, but without the time factor)
  • Essentially a percentage
  • Example: 10 males out of 30 students → 10/30 × 100 = 33.3%

3. Morbidity — Definition and Measures

Morbidity definedAny departure, subjective or objective, from a state of physical and psychological well-being. In practice it encompasses disease, injury, and disability. Also refers to the number of persons who are ill, periods of illness, or duration of illness.

Two key measures of morbidity frequency:

  1. Incidence — how many people become ill
  2. Prevalence — how many people are ill at a given time

4. Incidence

Definition: The number of new cases of disease in a defined population over a defined period of time.

Incidence Formula
Incidence = Number of new cases of disease in population over defined time / Number of persons at risk in population during that time

Key properties:

  • Only includes new cases in the numerator
  • Denominator = persons at risk at the start of the observation period
  • Incidence proportion = a measure of risk or probability of developing disease
  • Because new cases (numerator) are also in the denominator → incidence proportion is also a proportion

Denominator = Population at Risk

  • Must be restricted to those at risk of developing the disease
  • Example: For ovarian cancer — denominator must be women only
  • Exception: Malaria — general population can be used since all ages are exposed

Types:

  • Cumulative incidence (incidence proportion) — % infected per year
  • Incidence density (incidence rate) — number per person-time of observation
📝
Worked Example — Incidence Proportion (Risk):
100 of 189 diabetic men died during a 13-year follow-up.
Risk = 100/189 × 100 = 52.9%
📝
Worked Example — Attack Rate (Special form of incidence):
30 of 99 persons who ate potato salad at a picnic developed gastroenteritis.
Risk (attack rate) = 30/99 × 100 = 30.3%
📌
Malaria Incidence — How to Calculate (Eritrea 2015 Example):
Actual population at risk = Total population × % at risk
Incidence = Malaria cases / Actual population at risk × 1,000

5. Prevalence

Definition: The proportion of a population who have a particular disease at a specified point or over a specified period of time.

Prevalence Formula
Prevalence = Number of persons with the disease / Number of persons in the population

Two types:

TypeDefinition
Point prevalencePrevalence at a single point in time — a snapshot
Period prevalencePrevalence measured over an interval of time
Important Relationship
Prevalence = Incidence × Duration of disease
  • High prevalence may reflect: high incidence OR prolonged survival without cure OR both
  • Low prevalence may indicate: low incidence OR rapidly fatal disease OR rapid recovery

Prevalence is preferred for chronic diseases (e.g., diabetes) — long duration, difficult to pinpoint date of onset.

📝
Worked Example — Prevalence:
In Tana River district (pop. 50,000), a survey of 4,000 people found 200 positive for schistosomiasis.
Prevalence = 200/4,000 × 100 = 5%
📝
Worked Example — Prevalence (Multivitamin use):
468 of 1,150 women in Eldoret took multivitamins ≥4 times/week.
Prevalence = 468/1,150 × 100 = 40.7%

7. Measures of Association and Risk

Risk = the probability/likelihood of a (usually negative) outcome in a specified period of time.

Quantitative evidence of risk provides the basis for:

  • Public health action
  • Instituting interventions
  • Priority allocation of resources

Three Main Measures of Association:

  1. Relative Risk (RR)
  2. Odds Ratio (OR)
  3. Attributable Risk (AR)

8. The 2×2 Contingency Table

The foundation for calculating all measures of association. Know how to fill this in and use it.

9. Relative Risk (RR)

Definition: A ratio of risk comparing two groups based on their exposure status. Measures the strength of association.

Used in: Prospective studies (cohort studies)

RR Formula
RR = Incidence in exposed group / Incidence in unexposed group = [a / (a+b)] ÷ [c / (c+d)]

Interpreting RR:

RR valueInterpretation
RR = 1.0No association — risk identical in exposed and unexposed
RR > 1.0Positive association — increased risk in exposed group
RR < 1.0Negative association — decreased risk in exposed group (protective)
RR = 1.330% increased risk for exposed vs. unexposed
RR = 5Exposed are 5 times more likely to have the outcome
RR = 0.5Exposed are half as likely to have the outcome
📝
Worked Example — RR (E. coli and pink hamburger):
Ate hamburger: 23 ill / 33 total  |  Did not eat: 7 ill / 67 total
RR = (23/33) ÷ (7/67) = 0.697 ÷ 0.104 = 6.67
Interpretation: Those who ate the pink hamburger were 6.67 times more likely to develop E. coli infection.

10. Odds Ratio (OR)

Definition: The ratio of the odds of developing disease in exposed persons to the odds in unexposed persons.

Used in: Case-control studies (because population at risk is not known — you start with cases and controls)

OR Formula
OR = (a × d) / (b × c)

OR is similar to RR and is interpreted the same way:

  • OR = 1 → No association
  • OR > 1 → Positive association (increased risk)
  • OR < 1 → Negative association (decreased risk / protective)
📝
Worked Example — OR (MMR vaccine and autism):
MMR vaccine Yes: 130 autism cases / 115 non-cases = 245 total
MMR vaccine No: 120 autism cases / 135 non-cases = 255 total
OR = (130 × 135) / (115 × 120) = 17,550 / 13,800 = 1.27
Interpretation: Those vaccinated had 1.27 times the odds of autism compared to unvaccinated — a very weak association.

11. Attributable Risk (AR) / Risk Difference (RD)

Definition: The excess risk of disease in the exposed group that is directly attributable to the exposure — after accounting for background risk in the unexposed.

Also called: Risk Difference or Excess Risk

AR Formula
AR = I(exposed) − I(unexposed) Where I = Incidence
📝
Worked Example — Fast driving vs. slow driving:
Fast driving: 100 dead / 2,000 total → Risk = 0.050
Slow driving: 80 dead / 8,000 total → Risk = 0.010
AR (fast driving) = 0.050 − 0.010 = 0.040 (4 extra deaths per 100 due to fast driving)
📝
Worked Example — Drunk driving:
Drunk: 45 dead / 300 total → Risk = 0.150
Not drunk: 135 dead / 9,700 total → Risk = 0.014
AR (drunk driving) = 0.150 − 0.014 = 0.136

12. Attributable Fraction / Attributable Risk % (AR%)

Definition: AR expressed as a percentage of total risk in the exposed group. Answers: What proportion of disease among the exposed is due to the exposure?

Also called: Attributable proportion, Etiologic fraction

AF% Formula
AF% = [I(exposed) − I(unexposed)] / I(exposed) × 100 Or equivalently: AF% = 1 − (1/RR) For case-control studies: AF = 1 − (1/OR)
📝
Worked Example — Fast driving:
AF% = (0.050 − 0.010) / 0.050 × 100 = 80%
Meaning: 80% of deaths among fast drivers are attributable to fast driving.
📝
Worked Example — Drunk driving:
AF% = (0.150 − 0.014) / 0.150 × 100 = 91%
Meaning: 91% of deaths among drunk drivers are attributable to drunk driving.

13. Prevented Fraction (PF)

Used when: Exposure is associated with decreased risk (RR < 1) — i.e., a protective exposure (like a vaccine).

Answers: What proportion of potential cases were prevented by the exposure?

📝
Vaccine Efficacy Example (from slides):
Fluoridation of water — incidence of dental caries: 14.8% (fluoridated) vs. 21.3% (non-fluoridated)
PF = (21.3 − 14.8) / 21.3 = 30.5%
About 30% of caries that would have occurred were prevented by fluoridation.

14. Population Attributable Risk (PAR) and Population Attributable Fraction (PAF)

PAR Definition: The excess risk of disease in the total population attributable to the exposure. How much would disease decrease if the entire population were unexposed?

PAF / PAR% Definition: The proportion of cases in the total population attributable to the exposure, expressed as a percentage of total population risk.

PAF Formula (case-control studies)
PAF = (1 − 1/OR) × Pc Where Pc = percentage of cases who were exposed
🔑
Key application: Helps determine which exposures are most relevant to public health at the community level — where to put resources for greatest impact.
📝
Driving deaths conclusion (from slides):
44% of driving-related deaths in the population attributed to fast driving
22% of driving-related deaths in the population attributed to drunk driving
∴ Targeting fast driving would have a greater population-level impact

16. Tests of Statistical Significance

95% Confidence Interval (CI)

  • The range of values that has a 95% chance of containing the true RR or OR
  • To be statistically significant: the CI must NOT include 1.0
  • CI entirely above 1 → increased risk in exposed (positive association)
  • CI entirely below 1 → decreased risk in exposed (negative/protective association)
  • CI includes 1 → no significant association

p-value

  • The probability that the observed association occurred by chance alone
  • p < 0.05 → statistically significant (less than 5% chance it's due to chance)
  • Very small p-value → very unlikely to observe that RR/OR if there's no true association

17. Mortality Measures

  • Measure of frequency of death in a defined population during a specified interval
  • Expressed per 1,000 or 100,000 population
Mortality Rate Formula
Mortality rate = Deaths occurring during a given period / Population in which deaths occurred × 100

Denominator commonly = mid-year population (when based on vital statistics)

Crude Death Rate
Total number of deaths during a given time interval / Mid-interval population × 1000
Cause-Specific Death Rate
Number of deaths assigned to a specific cause during a given interval / Mid-interval population × 1000

Case Fatality Rate (CFR)

Definition: A measure of the severity (killing power) of a disease — proportion of diagnosed cases who die.

"Killing power of a disease"
CFR Formula
CFR = Number of deaths from a disease in a given time period / Number of diagnosed cases in the same period × 100

Worked Examples — CFR:

📝
Malaria (Siaya County, 1998):
20 deaths among 200 admitted children
CFR = 20/200 × 100 = 10%
📝
Ebola (West Africa at peak):
90 deaths among 100 admitted patients
CFR = 90/100 × 100 = 90%
Ebola is far more severe — CFR of 90% vs 10%

18. Person-Time

What is it? A unit combining persons and time for calculating incidence rates when individuals are followed for different lengths of time.

1 person at risk observed for 1 year = 1 person-year 1 person at risk observed for 1 month = 1 person-month Person-time has units: year⁻¹ or month⁻¹

Used in: Incidence density (rate) calculations, especially in cohort studies.
